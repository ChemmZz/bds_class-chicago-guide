01 — The Shift

What v1 got right — and what it got wrong

The first iteration worked. The data was solid. The routing algorithm was correct. The tag system produced genuinely useful combinations. The map interaction — click a card, pan to it; click a marker, scroll to the card — felt natural once you discovered it.

But v1 was also heavy. The dark aesthetic, the cyan glows, the inset shadows stacked three layers deep, the pill buttons with their lift animations — all of it competed with the data. The design was announcing itself. A guide that shouts about its own appearance is a guide that's distracted from its job.

"Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away."

There was also a practical problem: dark mode as default is wrong for outdoor daytime use. Chicago is a walking city. A tourist standing outside Willis Tower at noon, holding their phone up at full brightness, can't read deep navy. The japandy direction solves this by going light — warm paper, not blinding white.

02 — Japandy

間 — the meaningful interval between things

"Japandy" is shorthand for a compound aesthetic: Japanese minimalism crossed with Scandinavian warmth. The Japanese contribution is ma (間) — a concept with no direct English translation. It describes the productive space between objects. The pause between notes. The gap between buildings. Applied to UI, it means: the empty space is not empty. It carries meaning. It is designed.

In practice: `line-height: 1.8` instead of `1.6`. Card padding at `24px` instead of `16px`. One accent color — ink blue `#3d5a80` — used exactly where interaction needs to be signaled, and nowhere else.

The Scandinavian layer softens the austerity. Pure Japanese minimalism can read as cold. Scandinavian hygge adds warmth: the background is `#f7f5f0` — slightly toasted, not pure white. The surface is `#faf9f6`. The text is `#2c2925` — warm charcoal, not pure black. Every color leans toward paper and ink rather than screen and pixel.

03 — Removed

The art of subtraction

Most of the work in v2 was deletion. Here is what was removed and why:

Removed from v1

  • Dark tile CSS invert trick
  • Pill-shaped tag buttons
  • 4px colored left border on cards
  • Glows: box-shadow chains with opacity gradients
  • Chicago flag ★ ★ ★ ★ header decoration
  • Card hover translateX(3px) shift
  • Inset shadow on tag buttons at rest
  • Filled colored map markers
  • Route polyline at 2.5px weight

What replaced it

  • Light OSM tiles — used natively
  • Rectangular buttons, 1px border only
  • 6px color dot at top-left of card
  • 1px subtle drop shadow, no color
  • Nothing — the whitespace is sufficient
  • 1px border highlight on focus only
  • Transparent at rest, bottom border on active
  • Outline-circle markers, white interior
  • 1.5px ink-blue polyline, 50% opacity

Each removal was a specific decision, not a blanket minimization. The dark tile CSS trick (invert(1) hue-rotate(180deg)) is genuinely clever in v1, but on light ground it would require re-inverting, producing color artifacts. The pill buttons in v1 carry personality but also imply softness that doesn't match a city guide. Chicago's architecture is not soft. The rectangular button is more honest.

04 — Added

The new design vocabulary

The most significant addition is typographic. Cormorant Garamond at 300 weight — ultra-light — is a different beast than Playfair Display at 700. At large sizes it has a quality associated with Japanese typefaces designed for Western letterforms: each glyph holds more space around it, reads slower, carries more individual weight.

Headings Cormorant Garamond
Body DM Sans · weight 300
Labels DM MONO · 0.10em

DM Mono replaces Space Mono for labels and addresses. It's warmer — the letterforms are slightly rounder, less telegraphic. Space Mono reads as technical. DM Mono reads as handwritten-ish. For a city guide that values texture over efficiency, that distinction matters.

The tag colors are all desaturated by roughly 50% compared to v1. They still differentiate the six categories, but they don't compete with the basemap or the text. On light ground, v1's saturated amber and gold would vibrate against the cream background. The desaturated versions sit quietly.

The outline-circle map markers are the most consequential change. In v1, filled markers felt visually heavy — 28px colored discs scattered over the map. In v2, the outline circles let the basemap show through their white interior. You read the number; the map geography remains visible behind it.

05 — Interpretation

Same data, seen differently

The SPOTS array is identical between v1 and v2. The routing algorithm is identical. The tag filtering logic is identical. What changes is the interpretive frame — and that changes how the data reads.

In v1, the Jazz Showcase reads as a nightlife destination (dark, moody, the glowing yellow badge). In v2, it reads as a cultural institution (clean type, warm paper ground, restrained gold marker). Same words. Different weight. The design tells you how to feel about it before you read a word.

This is the argument for doing the same project twice. Not because one version is better — both are valid interpretations of the same city — but because the second iteration reveals what the first one was assuming. The dark aesthetic assumed Chicago at night, a certain kind of tourism, a certain kind of traveler. The japandy iteration assumes something else: afternoon light, slow walking, unhurried attention.

Design is not decoration. It is the argument you make about what something is.

The plan is to keep iterating. Each version will use the same 25 spots and the same routing algorithm. Each version will be a different argument about Chicago. The gallery page at the root holds all of them.
